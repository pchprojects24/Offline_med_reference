ACLS Special Situation

Hypothermia

Entry criteria (suspect hypothermia)

  • Core temperature below normal OR high clinical suspicion (cold exposure, immersion, prolonged cold environment)
  • May present with altered mental status, bradycardia, hypotension, slow respirations, dysrhythmias
  • Assume hypothermia in cold/immersion exposures until proven otherwise

Immediate actions (first moments)

  • Handle gently; avoid unnecessary movement (arrhythmia risk)
  • Remove from cold/wet environment; remove wet clothing if possible
  • Prevent further heat loss:
    • Dry patient, insulate, cover head/neck as appropriate
    • Warm environment if available
  • ABCs:
    • Airway: protect as needed
    • Breathing: provide oxygen; ventilate if inadequate
    • Circulation: check pulse carefully (see below), attach monitor/defib
  • Obtain temperature if available (core preferred). If not available: treat based on clinical severity.

Pulse/respiratory assessment (avoid premature "no pulse")

  • In severe hypothermia, pulse and respirations may be slow and difficult to detect
  • Check for a central pulse and breathing for a prolonged period if needed (VERIFY local teaching time; commonly longer than usual)
  • If truly pulseless → start CPR

Classification (practical, for decisions)

  • Mild: awake/shivering (typically ~32–35°C) (VERIFY thresholds per protocol)
  • Moderate: reduced shivering, altered, bradycardic (typically ~28–32°C) (VERIFY)
  • Severe: unconscious, very slow/absent respirations, high dysrhythmia risk, possible arrest (typically <28°C) (VERIFY)

Rewarming strategy (match to severity)

1) Mild (alert, shivering)

  • Passive + active external warming:
    • Insulation, warm environment, warm dry blankets
    • Warm sweet drinks if fully awake and not nauseated (avoid alcohol)
  • Monitor and reassess

2) Moderate to severe (altered, not reliably shivering, hemodynamic instability)

  • Active external warming:
    • Warm blankets, forced-air warming if available
    • Warm packs to trunk (avoid direct skin burns; avoid aggressive limb warming first if very cold)
  • Active internal/supportive measures (as available):
    • Warmed IV fluids if available (do not delay transport/evac planning to obtain)
    • Warm humidified oxygen if available
  • Treat hypoglycemia and dehydration as appropriate

Arrhythmias and monitoring (common in hypothermia)

  • Bradycardia is often physiologic; avoid unnecessary pacing unless clearly unstable and refractory to warming (VERIFY local approach)
  • Atrial dysrhythmias may occur and can resolve with warming
  • Avoid rough handling; minimize invasive procedures unless necessary

If cardiac arrest occurs (hypothermic arrest)

  • Begin high-quality CPR and follow ACLS with hypothermia modifications
  • Defibrillation:
    • If VF/pVT: attempt defibrillation per standard approach
    • If repeated shocks unsuccessful in severe hypothermia, prioritize CPR + rewarming and follow local protocol on shock frequency (VERIFY)
  • Medications:
    • In severe hypothermia, drug metabolism is slowed
    • Follow local protocol regarding timing/withholding of IV meds below certain temperatures (VERIFY)
  • Continue resuscitation while rewarming when feasible ("not dead until warm and resuscitated" principle; apply per local policy/context)

Key complications to anticipate

  • Hypoglycemia
  • Dehydration/volume depletion
  • Coexisting trauma (falls, immersion injury)
  • Frostbite or non-freezing cold injuries
  • Electrolyte derangements and acidosis in prolonged exposure

Exit paths

  • Mild/moderate with improvement → continue warming, monitor, reassess, plan observation/transfer as appropriate
  • Moderate/severe or unstable → early escalation/transfer planning; continue active warming and close monitoring
  • Arrest or recurrent instability → continue ACLS + hypothermia modifications and prioritize definitive rewarming capability
