ACLS Special Situation

Anaphylaxis

Entry criteria (treat if suspected)

  • Sudden illness after likely allergen exposure WITH any of:
    • Airway: throat tightness, hoarseness/stridor, tongue/lip swelling
    • Breathing: wheeze, increased work of breathing, hypoxemia
    • Circulation: hypotension, collapse, syncope, shock
    • OR skin/mucosal features (hives, flushing, angioedema) PLUS respiratory or cardiovascular involvement
  • If in doubt and there is airway/breathing/circulation compromise: treat as anaphylaxis

Immediate actions (first moments)

  • Call for help; assign roles; prepare for rapid deterioration
  • Position:
    • Keep patient supine (or left lateral if pregnant); elevate legs if hypotensive
    • Avoid sudden standing/sitting (can precipitate collapse)
  • Remove trigger if possible (stop infusion, remove stinger)
  • Airway/Breathing:
    • High-flow oxygen as needed
    • Prepare early for airway edema (difficult airway risk)
  • Circulation:
    • Establish IV access; start fluid resuscitation if hypotensive
  • Monitoring:
    • Continuous ECG, SpO₂, BP; consider waveform capnography if ventilated

First-line medication (do not delay)

  • Epinephrine IM (preferred)
    • Adult: 0.5 mg IM (0.5 mL of 1 mg/mL [1:1000]) into mid-anterolateral thigh
    • Repeat every 5–15 minutes as needed based on symptoms and response
    • If an auto-injector is the only option available: use it immediately and repeat as per device availability/clinical response

Core cycle (repeat and reassess)

  1. Give IM epinephrine promptly
  2. Reassess airway/breathing/circulation within minutes
  3. Repeat IM epinephrine if ongoing airway/breathing compromise or hypotension
  4. Treat shock aggressively with IV fluids
  5. Escalate early if refractory (see below)

Fluids (for hypotension/shock)

  • Give rapid isotonic crystalloid bolus; reassess frequently
  • Ongoing shock may require repeated boluses (VERIFY typical adult bolus volumes per your local protocol)

Adjuncts (NOT substitutes for epinephrine)

  • Bronchospasm:
    • Inhaled beta-agonist (e.g., salbutamol) for wheeze/bronchospasm
  • Cutaneous symptoms:
    • H1 antihistamine can help hives/itch but does not treat airway/shock
  • Steroids:
    • May be used as an adjunct; do not delay epinephrine (benefit for biphasic reaction prevention is uncertain)
  • Consider glucagon if patient on beta-blocker with refractory hypotension/bronchospasm (VERIFY dosing/prep per protocol)

Refractory anaphylaxis (escalation)

  • Definition (practical): persistent respiratory compromise or hypotension despite:
    • Repeated IM epinephrine (e.g., 2–3 doses) AND
    • Adequate fluid resuscitation
  • Actions:
    • Begin epinephrine IV infusion if trained/equipped (VERIFY local infusion concentration/rate and pump availability)
    • Seek senior consult early; prioritize airway control if worsening edema/stridor
    • AVOID IV epinephrine bolus dosing unless in a monitored setting with expert support (higher adverse event risk)

Airway red flags (act early)

  • Rapidly progressive tongue/oropharyngeal swelling
  • Stridor/voice change
  • Inability to swallow secretions
  • Worsening respiratory distress despite IM epinephrine
  • If present: prepare for early advanced airway with best available expertise and backup plan

Observation / recurrence risk

  • Symptoms can recur (biphasic reaction). Observe and reassess after stabilization.
  • Provide discharge planning when appropriate:
    • Trigger avoidance + follow-up
    • Epinephrine auto-injector planning if applicable (VERIFY local pathway)

Exit paths

  • Improved and stable after treatment → monitor/observe, document, plan follow-up and prevention
  • Persistent instability or airway risk → escalate/transfer planning (at-sea: early discussion for MEDEVAC triggers)
  • Cardiorespiratory arrest → transition to ACLS arrest algorithms
