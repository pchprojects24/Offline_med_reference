Adult Cardiac Arrest - Shockable (VF / pulseless VT)

Scrollable checklist for shockable rhythm management.

Entry criteria

  • Unresponsive
  • Not breathing normally
  • No palpable pulse
  • Monitor shows VF or pulseless VT

Immediate actions

  • Start high-quality CPR: 100-120/min, depth 5-6 cm, full recoil, minimize interruptions.
  • Apply monitor/defibrillator as soon as available.
  • Provide oxygen; attach waveform capnography if available.
  • Establish IV access (IO if IV delayed).

Shockable rhythm cycle (repeat)

  1. Defibrillate (biphasic: follow manufacturer-recommended energy).
  2. Resume CPR immediately after shock (no pulse/rhythm check).
  3. CPR for 2 minutes (optimize compressions/ventilation; prepare airway adjuncts).
  4. Rhythm check:
    • If still VF/pVT → shock and resume CPR immediately.
    • If non-shockable → transition to PEA/Asystole pathway.
    • If organized rhythm with pulse → treat as ROSC and move to post-cardiac arrest care.

Medications (during CPR)

  • Epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO:
    • Give after the second shock.
    • Repeat every 3-5 minutes.
  • Antiarrhythmic for persistent VF/pVT:
    • Amiodarone 300 mg IV/IO bolus; may give additional 150 mg IV/IO if refractory.
    • Alternative if amiodarone unavailable: lidocaine per local protocol.

During cycles (quality checkpoints)

  • Rotate compressors every 2 minutes.
  • Avoid pauses > 10 seconds.
  • Avoid hyperventilation; ensure adequate chest rise.
  • Confirm advanced airway placement with waveform capnography if placed.
  • Use ETCO2 trends (low values suggest CPR quality/physiology issues).

Reversible causes (address concurrently)

Actively look for and treat Hs & Ts:

  • Hypoxia
  • Hypovolemia
  • Hydrogen ion (acidosis)
  • Hypo-/hyperkalemia
  • Hypothermia
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Cardiac tamponade
  • Toxins
  • Thrombosis (coronary or pulmonary)

Exit paths

  • ROSC → Post-Cardiac Arrest Care page.
  • Non-shockable rhythm → PEA/Asystole page.
  • Persistent VF/pVT → continue shock/CPR/med cycles; seek expert support if available.

Verify against current Canadian/AHA guidelines. This is a personal quick reference only.
