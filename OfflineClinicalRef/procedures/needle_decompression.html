Breathing Procedure

Needle decompression (tension pneumothorax)

Indications

  • Suspected tension pneumothorax with clinical deterioration, especially with trauma/positive pressure ventilation
  • High suspicion when there is:
    • Severe respiratory distress + hypoxemia
    • Hemodynamic compromise/shock not explained otherwise
    • Unilateral absent/decreased breath sounds (supportive but not required)
    • Distended neck veins (may be absent), tracheal deviation (late/unreliable)
    • Sudden deterioration after PPV, chest injury, or known pneumothorax

Contraindications / cautions

  • No absolute contraindication when true tension is suspected and patient is crashing
  • Avoid delay for imaging when unstable
  • Anticipate failure if catheter too short or placement incorrect; be prepared to repeat or progress to chest tube

Equipment

  • Antiseptic prep; gloves/eye protection
  • Large-bore catheter (device length matters); or commercial decompression needle if stocked
  • Occlusive dressing
  • Tape/securement
  • Optional: extension set / 3-way stopcock per local practice
  • Monitor: SpO2, ECG, BP; ETCO2 if ventilated
  • Chest tube kit available if within capability and indicated

Steps (checklist)

1) Confirm high suspicion + treat immediately if unstable

  • Do not wait for imaging in a crashing patient
  • Provide oxygen; support ventilation as needed
  • Prepare for rapid reassessment and escalation

2) Choose site per local protocol (common options)

  • 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line (2nd ICS MCL) OR
  • 4th/5th intercostal space, anterior axillary line (4th/5th ICS AAL)
  • Use the approach your system trains and your equipment supports

3) Landmark and prep

  • Identify rib spaces carefully; aim just above the rib to avoid neurovascular bundle
  • Clean skin; maintain control of needle/catheter

4) Decompress

  • Insert perpendicular (or per your protocol for that site) until pleural entry is felt
  • Advance catheter fully; remove needle; secure catheter
  • Listen/feel for rush of air (may be absent); reassess clinical response

5) Immediate reassessment

  • Look for improvement:
    • Reduced respiratory distress, improved SpO2, improved BP/perfusion
    • Improved compliance if ventilated
  • If no improvement and suspicion remains:
    • Consider catheter failure (kink, short length, wrong site)
    • Reattempt at alternate site per protocol and/or proceed to chest tube if within capability

Confirmation / success criteria

  • Clinical improvement (primary)
  • Improved ventilation compliance/ETCO2 trend (if ventilated)
  • Improved hemodynamics and oxygenation
  • Do not rely solely on "rush of air"

Complications + immediate management

  • Failure to decompress (most common)
    • Reassess site/landmarks; consider longer catheter; alternate site; chest tube
  • Bleeding / vessel injury
    • Direct pressure; reassess; escalate
  • Lung injury or hemothorax
    • Monitor; prepare definitive management
  • Infection (rare in acute setting)
    • Use antisepsis; definitive care follow-up

Aftercare / monitoring

  • Continuous monitoring; reassess frequently
  • Treat as a temporizing measure—plan for definitive management (often chest tube) when possible
  • Secure catheter; document site, side, time, response, complications
  • If transporting/evac: communicate decompression performed and patient response
