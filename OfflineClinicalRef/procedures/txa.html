Circulation / Hemorrhage Procedure

TXA administration (trauma)

Indications

  • Suspected or confirmed major hemorrhage due to trauma where hyperfibrinolysis is a concern, especially if:
    • Hemorrhagic shock or ongoing significant bleeding, OR
    • High risk of significant bleeding based on mechanism/injuries
  • Time-sensitive: benefit is greatest when given early after injury (VERIFY your local time window; commonly "as soon as possible" and within hours)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Use per local protocol and medical direction
  • Known hypersensitivity to TXA
  • Caution / risk-benefit considerations (VERIFY local protocol):
    • Active intravascular clotting / recent thromboembolic event history
    • Isolated head injury without systemic bleeding (protocol-dependent)
    • Significant renal impairment (dose considerations in some contexts)
  • Do not delay definitive hemorrhage control/resuscitation to administer TXA

Equipment

  • TXA vial/ampoule per your stock concentration
  • IV/IO access + flush
  • Syringes/needles, minibag or syringe pump setup as per local practice
  • Labeling and documentation supplies
  • Monitoring: ECG, BP, SpO2

Steps (checklist)

  1. Identify candidate early
    • Trauma + suspected significant bleeding / hemorrhagic shock risk
    • Start as early as possible once criteria met (do not wait for labs)
  2. Administer TXA per local protocol (VERIFY)
    • Common trauma regimen used in many systems (VERIFY):
      • 1 g IV over ~10 minutes, then 1 g infusion over ~8 hours
      • Some systems use a 2 g IV bolus approach (protocol-dependent)
    • Route:
      • IV preferred; IO acceptable if IV not available (follow local guidance)
  3. Continue hemorrhage management in parallel
    • Control bleeding (tourniquet, packing, pressure dressings)
    • Resuscitation per protocol (fluids/blood products availability varies)
    • Prevent hypothermia; monitor for deterioration

Confirmation / success criteria

  • TXA delivered without adverse reaction
  • Ongoing reassessment of hemorrhage/shock response occurs (TXA is adjunctive; not a "response marker" itself)

Complications + immediate management

  • Hypersensitivity reaction (rare)
    • Stop administration; treat per anaphylaxis protocol if indicated
  • IV site irritation / infusion issues
    • Check patency, slow/adjust administration per protocol
  • Thromboembolic risk (contextual/rare in acute dosing; monitor clinically)
    • Maintain vigilance; follow local reporting/escalation pathways

Aftercare / monitoring

  • Document:
    • Indication, time from injury (estimate), time given, dose/regimen, route (IV/IO), adverse events
  • Ensure handover includes:
    • TXA given, regimen started/completed, and remaining infusion needs (if applicable)
