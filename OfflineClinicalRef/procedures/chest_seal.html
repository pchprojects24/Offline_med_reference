Breathing Procedure

Chest seal / open pneumothorax

Indications

  • Open chest wound with air movement through the wound ("sucking" wound) or suspected open pneumothorax
  • Penetrating chest trauma with respiratory distress where an open wound is present
  • Temporizing management while preparing definitive care (e.g., chest tube) or transport

Contraindications / cautions

  • No absolute contraindication when managing an open chest wound
  • Risk: an occlusive dressing can convert an open pneumothorax into a tension pneumothorax
    • Requires close monitoring and readiness to "burp"/lift edge or decompress per protocol if deterioration occurs

Equipment

  • Commercial vented chest seal (preferred if available)
  • If no commercial device: occlusive dressing (e.g., petroleum gauze/large adhesive) + tape
  • Trauma shears, gloves, gauze to wipe/clean skin
  • Suction ready (blood/secretions)
  • Monitoring: SpO2, ECG, BP; ETCO2 if ventilated
  • Needle decompression kit available if patient deteriorates (per scope/protocol)

Steps (checklist)

1) Initial actions

  • Expose chest fully; identify wound(s) (entry/exit)
  • Control external bleeding with direct pressure as needed (do not occlude the wound with bulky packing intended for extremities)
  • Suction/clear blood and wipe skin quickly to improve seal adherence

2) Apply seal

  • Commercial vented seal (preferred):
    • Center over wound; adhere firmly on all sides
    • Ensure vent is not blocked by clothing/blood
  • If improvised occlusive dressing is the only option:
    • Apply an occlusive dressing large enough to cover the wound
    • Secure firmly; if your local protocol teaches a "burp-able" edge, apply accordingly (VERIFY local approach)

3) Reassess immediately and continuously

  • Assess: work of breathing, SpO2, breath sounds, chest rise, perfusion, mental status
  • Watch for signs of tension physiology after sealing:
    • Worsening respiratory distress/hypoxemia
    • Increasing ventilatory pressures (if ventilated)
    • Hemodynamic compromise/shock
    • Unilateral absent breath sounds or rapid deterioration

4) If deterioration suggests tension pneumothorax

  • If using an occlusive seal: briefly lift an edge ("burp") to allow trapped air to escape if trained/appropriate
  • Proceed to needle decompression per protocol if tension suspected, then plan definitive care (often chest tube)

Confirmation / success criteria

  • Reduced air movement through wound and improved ventilation/oxygenation (clinical improvement)
  • No progressive deterioration suggesting tension physiology

Complications + immediate management

  • Conversion to tension pneumothorax
    • Burp/lift edge (if appropriate) and decompress per protocol; escalate to chest tube if available/indicated
  • Poor adhesion/leak (blood/sweat/hair)
    • Wipe/dry, reapply, reinforce edges; consider alternative seal/dressing
  • Ongoing bleeding
    • Direct pressure around wound and rapid escalation; treat shock

Aftercare / monitoring

  • Keep seal visible; reassess frequently and after any movement/transfer
  • Prepare for definitive management/transport
  • Document wound location, seal type, response, and any deterioration/interventions
