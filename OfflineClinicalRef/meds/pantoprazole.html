Pantoprazole

Stocked on board

  • Pantoprazole 40mg Tablets
  • Pantoprazole 40mg Injection

Adult dosing

GERD / reflux symptoms or erosive esophagitis (common use)

  • 40 mg PO once daily (typical course 4–8 weeks; reassess)

Suspected upper GI bleeding / significant ulcer bleed (protocol-dependent; higher-acuity use)

  • IV dosing varies by protocol; common approaches include:
  • 40–80 mg IV, then 8 mg/hour IV infusion (where capability/protocol exists)

Stress ulcer prophylaxis / high-risk gastritis (context-dependent)

  • 40 mg PO or IV once daily (use local protocol)

Indications

  • GERD, erosive esophagitis; peptic ulcer disease (provider-directed)
  • Treatment/prevention of GI bleeding related to acid-mediated injury (protocol-dependent)
  • When an IV PPI is required because PO is not feasible or patient is high acuity

Contraindications / cautions

Contraindications

  • Hypersensitivity to pantoprazole/PPIs
  • Concomitant use with rilpivirine (avoid)

Cautions (especially prolonged or high-dose use)

  • C. difficile–associated diarrhea risk; pneumonia risk in some settings
  • Hypomagnesemia (especially with prolonged use), B12 deficiency (long-term), fracture risk (long-term)
  • Possible interaction considerations with clopidogrel (follow local guidance)

Adverse effects

  • Headache, diarrhea, abdominal pain, nausea
  • IV: injection-site irritation/phlebitis (can occur)
  • Rare but important: severe diarrhea (consider C. difficile), allergic reaction

Preparation & administration

PO tablets

  • Swallow whole; do not crush/chew

IV injection/infusion (40 mg vial)

  • Reconstitute each 40 mg vial with 10 mL of 0.9% sodium chloride to yield ~4 mg/mL
  • Administration options (protocol-dependent):
  • IV injection of reconstituted solution
  • IV infusion: further dilute and administer by infusion over ~15 minutes (see monograph for exact dilution examples)
  • Use a dedicated line or flush before/after; do not run simultaneously with other IV solutions in the same line

Monitoring

  • Symptom response (pain, reflux, hematemesis/melena if GI bleed context)
  • For GI bleed contexts: hemodynamics, Hgb trends if available, ongoing bleeding signs
  • With prolonged use: consider magnesium/B12 risk if clinically relevant

At-sea considerations

  • In suspected GI bleed, document timing/dose and reassess frequently; early escalation/evac planning if unstable or recurrent bleeding
  • IV prep needs reliable IV access, compatible diluent, and clear labeling to avoid mix-ups
  • If clopidogrel/ASA are being used, consider GI protection strategy per protocol and bleeding risk
