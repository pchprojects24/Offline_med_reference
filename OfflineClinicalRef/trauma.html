Trauma / ATLS

Primary survey checklist for initial trauma assessment and early life-saving actions.

Use this page for

  • Major blunt or penetrating trauma
  • Patients with abnormal vital signs, altered mental status, or significant mechanism of injury
  • Any trauma patient who may need rapid procedure support or evacuation planning

Immediate actions

  1. Scene + team safety
    • Confirm hazards are controlled before committing to care.
    • Use PPE and request additional help early if available.
  2. Quick first look
    • Massive external bleeding?
    • Not breathing or agonal breathing?
    • Trapped, unstable, or deteriorating patient?
  3. Start the primary survey
    • Treat life threats as they are found.
    • Repeat the primary survey after each major intervention or status change.

Primary survey (ABCDE)

A — Airway with cervical spine protection

  • Assess ability to speak, airway patency, gurgling, stridor, vomit, blood, facial trauma, and decreasing level of consciousness.
  • Maintain manual cervical spine protection when mechanism suggests spinal injury.
  • Open/clear the airway and suction as needed.
  • If basic positioning and adjuncts are not enough, move quickly to advanced airway strategy.

B — Breathing and chest

  • Assess respiratory effort, rate, oxygenation, chest rise, chest wounds, tracheal deviation, and breath sounds if obtainable.
  • Give oxygen and support ventilation early when work of breathing is high or mental status is worsening.
  • Look specifically for tension pneumothorax, open pneumothorax, flail chest, and severe hypoventilation.
  • If the patient deteriorates after chest trauma or positive-pressure ventilation, act on tension physiology immediately.

C — Circulation and hemorrhage control

  • Look for external bleeding first; expose enough to find the source.
  • Assess pulse quality, skin signs, capillary refill, mental status trend, and overall shock picture.
  • Control extremity bleeding with direct pressure or tourniquet; control junctional bleeding with wound packing and pressure.
  • Establish IV/IO access early if shock, major injury, or time-critical medication delivery is likely.
  • Consider TXA early in major traumatic hemorrhage per local protocol.

D — Disability / neurologic status

  • Check responsiveness, pupils, gross motor deficits, seizure activity, and glucose when relevant and available.
  • Trending mental status is often an early marker of shock, hypoxia, or intracranial injury.
  • Protect the airway early if consciousness is declining.

E — Exposure / environment

  • Fully expose when needed to find hidden injuries, then prevent heat loss immediately.
  • Reassess the back, axillae, groin, and under dressings when feasible.
  • Hypothermia worsens trauma outcomes; keep the patient warm throughout care and transport.

Critical findings that should change your pace

  • Airway obstruction, inability to oxygenate/ventilate, or rapidly falling mental status
  • Suspected tension pneumothorax or open chest wound
  • Uncontrolled external hemorrhage or shock despite initial measures
  • Pelvic instability, traumatic amputation, penetrating torso injury, or multisystem trauma
  • Persistent hypotension, tachycardia, hypoxia, or worsening neurologic exam

Next steps after the primary survey

  • If unstable, continue resuscitation, repeat the primary survey, and escalate evacuation planning early.
  • If temporarily stabilized, proceed to focused history, secondary survey, splinting, pain control, and serial reassessment.
  • Document times, interventions, responses, and trends for handoff.
